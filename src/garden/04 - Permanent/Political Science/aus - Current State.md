## 1 . Human‑development & broad prosperity

> Australia is still a very high-income, high-HDI nation, but its relative place in the global top tier has edged down because peer countries—especially in Northern Europe and East Asia—have improved faster on innovation, environmental metrics and productivity per hour.

## 2 . Distribution: income, wealth & housing

> While average wealth has surged, the spoils have skewed upward: disposable-income inequality is back to late-1990-s levels, the wealth Gini has risen, and housing has shifted from “seriously” to “severely” unaffordable—mainly due to tax settings that inflate property demand.

| Metric                                 | 1990/00s                                                                                                                                                            | Mid‑2020s                                                                                                                                                                                      | Trend                                       |
| ------------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Household income Gini (after tax/transfer) | 0.292 in 1996‑97 ([ABS](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/measuring-what-matters/measuring-what-matters-themes-and-indicators/prosperous/income-and-wealth-inequality)) |  0.323 in 2019‑20; prelim. 0.326 in 2021‑22 ([ABS](https://www.abs.gov.au/statistics/measuring-what-matters/measuring-what-matters-themes-and-indicators/prosperous/income-and-wealth-inequality)) | Inequality widened                          |

### Key policy drivers

* 1999‑2004 — 50 % capital‑gains‑tax discount & full negative‑gearing (Howard–Costello).
  Treasury’s 2023‑24 Tax Expenditures & Insights Statement puts rental‑loss deductions at A$27 bn p.a. ([TEIS PDF](https://treasury.gov.au/sites/default/files/2024-01/p2024-489823-teis.pdf)); media summary ([Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/australia-news/2024/jan/31/cost-of-negative-gearing-and-other-rental-deductions-soaring-australian-treasury-data-reveals)).

* Superannuation guarantee (Hawke/Keating 1992‑2002, now 11.5 % and rising to 12 %).
  Concessional super tax treatment costs A$48 bn p.a. – same Treasury TEIS report.

* National Competition Policy (1995‑2005) lifted multifactor‑productivity by ~2.5 ppts and real incomes by ~A$7 000 per household ([Productivity Commission NCP Inquiry](https://www.pc.gov.au/inquiries/completed/national-competition-policy)).

## 3 . Education, science & innovation

> Australia’s school-age STEM performance and national R&D effort have slipped from clear OECD leadership in the early-2000s to around the middle of the pack, reflecting stagnant public research funding, volatile R&D incentives and persistent teacher-supply gaps.

| Indicator | 2000s peak | Latest | Comment |
|---|---|---|---|
| PISA mathematics | 524 (2003) ([OECD PISA archive](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/indicators/mathematics-performance-pisa.html)) | 479 (2022) ([OECD country note](https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/pisa-2022-results-volume-i-and-ii-country-notes_ed6fbcc5-en/australia_e9346d47-en.html)) | ≈ ¾ school‑year decline |
| PISA science | 527 (2006) ([OECD](https://www.oecd.org/pisa/)) | 507 (2022) (same source) | Decline |
| Gross R&D spending (GERD % GDP) | 1.79 % (2008) ([OECD data](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/indicators/gross-domestic-spending-on-r-d.html)) | 1.68 % (2022) ([OECD](https://www.oecd.org/en/data/indicators/gross-domestic-spending-on-r-d.html)) | Lagging investment |
| Global Innovation Index | 18‑th (2009) ([GII archive](https://www.wipo.int/gii-ranking/en/australia)) | 25‑th (2022) ([GII 2022 PDF](https://www.wipo.int/edocs/pubdocs/en/wipo_pub_2000_2022/au.pdf)) | Rank slipped |

Long‑run impact factors

* Demand‑driven university funding (2010) boosted enrolments but real research block‑grants slid.
* Repeated tweaks to the R&D tax offset (2013‑20) created policy uncertainty, pushing business BERD lower.
* STEM‑curriculum and teacher‑supply reforms have yet to reverse the PISA decline.

## 4 . Macroeconomic resilience & fiscal capacity

> Policy frameworks (floating dollar, independent RBA, Future Fund) have kept debt low and smoothed shocks, but a a structural deficit still lingers and climate-policy reversals have added uncertainty—signalling strong short-run resilience yet weaker long-run discipline.

* 27‑year recession‑free run (1991‑2019) underpinned by a floating AUD, independent RBA inflation target, the 2000 GST swap and a historic terms‑of‑trade boom.
* Future Fund portfolio worth A$224.9 bn (30 Jun 2024) ([Future Fund update](https://www.futurefund.gov.au/-/media/E2E0BFA0DF574ADA8C3D31B9245C595F.ashx)) offsets public‑service super liabilities.
* Carbon price (2012) & repeal (2014) — ACCC found most cost savings passed through to consumers but NEM emissions rose 1.6 % post‑repeal ([ACCC repeal report PDF](https://www.accc.gov.au/system/files/carbon%20report%20-%20updated%20280714%20%28D2014-00100167%29_FA.pdf)).
* Gross debt (~36 % GDP) is low by OECD norms, yet a structural deficit of 2‑3 % GDP persists because bracket‑creep and commodity royalties were traded for large tax concessions in the 2000s.

## 5 . International contribution & soft power

>Australia still ranks respectably on the Good Country Index, yet its climate reputation and diplomatic presence no longer match its economic weight, causing a relative soft-power fade versus Nordic and Canadian peers.

* Good Country Index rank 18 (2022) — below most Nordic and Canadian peers ([GCI source](https://goodcountry.org/index/source-data)).
* Official‑development assistance (ODA) flat at 0.19 % GNI, far below the UN 0.7 % norm.
* Diplomatic footprint has thinned (embassy closures) even as Indo‑Pacific strategic competition intensified.
